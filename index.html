Are Electric Vehicles Really Economical?
A Global Data Investigation

Chanwoo Shin, Jinseong Choi, Daeyeong Mun
George Mason University Korea  ·  CDS 301/501, Spring 2026
EVs save you money.
Obviously.
Or do they? We ran the numbers — globally.
↓  scroll to find out
Abstract

Electric vehicles are marketed as the economical future of personal transportation. But the real story is more complicated. Using cost data across vehicle segments, ten major global markets, 10-year ownership timelines, battery degradation profiles, and mileage scenarios, this project asks a simple question: when — and for whom — does an EV actually become the cheaper choice? The answer depends heavily on where you live, how much you drive, and how long you keep the car.

Chapter 1 — The Sticker Shock

Let's start with the obvious problem.

Before you save a single dollar on gas, you have to buy the thing. And EVs cost more — in every segment, across every market.

+$12K The average EV premium over a comparable gas vehicle — before any government credit.

For a Compact SUV, that's $40,000 vs. $32,000. That $8,000 gap has to be earned back through savings before an EV can be called "economical."

"But wait — what about the $7,500 federal tax credit?"

Fair point. The U.S. Inflation Reduction Act offers up to $7,500 off eligible EVs, narrowing the Compact SUV gap to just $500.

✓ Chapter 1 Verdict After subsidies, EVs reach near price parity in the Compact and Sedan segments. Trucks and large SUVs still carry a $7,500–$15,000 premium even after credits.
Chapter 2 — The Daily Math

OK. But what about every trip to the pump?

This is where EVs genuinely shine — at least in most countries. But how big the savings are depends almost entirely on where you live.

83% Cheaper per km in China and Canada vs. an equivalent gas car.
62% Still cheaper in Germany — one of the toughest EV markets for running costs.
✓ Chapter 2 Verdict On running costs alone, EVs win everywhere — decisively. The question is whether daily savings are large enough to recoup the upfront premium.
Chapter 3 — The Long Game

Tesla Model Y vs. Toyota RAV4: who wins over 10 years?

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) combines depreciation, fuel, maintenance, and insurance — tracked year by year from the moment you drive off the lot.

Figure 4 · Line Chart
10-Year Cumulative Operating Cost: Tesla Model Y vs. RAV4 (USD)
Here's the uncomfortable truth: the RAV4 Gas is cheaper every single year.

With similar net purchase prices after subsidy ($32,500 EV vs $32,000 Gas), the EV's higher depreciation means it costs more annually — $11,108 in year 1 vs. the RAV4's $8,852. Then Year 8 hits: an $8,000 battery replacement blows out any accumulated advantage.

⚠ Chapter 3 Verdict In the U.S. at average mileage, the EV does not break even within 10 years on a pure TCO basis — even after subsidy. Higher depreciation and battery replacement risk are the culprits. But this changes dramatically based on how much you drive.
Chapter 4 — Mileage Is Everything

Drive more, and the EV math flips.

An EV's savings come from fuel costs. The more you drive, the more you save on electricity vs. gasoline. At low mileage, high depreciation kills the EV's advantage. At high mileage, the EV pulls decisively ahead.

5,000 mi/yr
EV costs $1,675 more than gas over 5 years. EV loses.

✓ Chapter 4 Verdict Break-even mileage is roughly 10,000 miles/year. Average U.S. drivers do ~15,000 — comfortably in EV-advantage territory. High-mileage commuters and fleet operators benefit the most.
Chapter 5 — The Government Factor

Subsidies can make or break the calculation.

The U.S. $7,500 credit is just one of many government levers. Norway's VAT exemption is worth ~$9,500. South Korea offers ~$6,200. Some countries give nothing. We modeled what happens to 5-year TCO as the incentive amount changes.

Figure 6 · Line Chart
5-Year TCO vs. Incentive Amount: EV vs. Gas Break-Even (USD)
$5,000 The minimum subsidy at which the EV becomes cheaper than gas over a 5-year period at average U.S. mileage.
⚠ Chapter 5 Verdict Without at least $5,000 in government support, an EV is not economically competitive at average mileage. This explains why EV adoption is highest in heavily subsidized markets.
Chapter 6 — The Battery Question

Nobody talks about this enough.

Battery degradation is the EV's Achilles heel for long-term ownership. Frequent DC fast-charging at high power degrades battery capacity roughly twice as fast as home slow-charging.

Figure 7 · Bar Chart

A battery at 76% capacity means 24% less range — and a potential $8,000 replacement bill.
✗ Chapter 6 Risk Battery degradation is a genuine long-term cost risk — especially for high-mileage drivers who rely on fast charging. The potential $8,000+ replacement is modeled into the Year 8 TCO spike in Chapter 3.
Conclusion

So — is the EV really economical?

The honest answer: it depends on three things.

✓ EV Wins If… You drive 15,000+ miles/year · Your government offers $5,000+ subsidy · You have cheap home electricity · You avoid frequent DC fast charging · You plan to keep the car 7+ years
✗ EV Loses If… You drive under 10,000 miles/year · No significant subsidy is available · You rely on expensive public charging · You live in an extreme climate · You plan to sell within 5 years

The structural trend is clear: battery costs are falling, fuel costs are rising, and government mandates are tightening ICE emissions. The question is no longer if EVs will be economical — it's whether they already are for you, today.

For the average global driver — yes.
Barely. And only with the right conditions.
Methodology & Data Processing

How we built this

All visualizations were generated using Python (pandas, matplotlib) for static charts and Chart.js for the interactive web layer. Vehicle cost data reflects 2024 U.S. market pricing. Regional running costs are based on 2024 annual average electricity and gasoline prices converted to USD at prevailing exchange rates. The TCO model assumes 15,000 miles/year, a Compact SUV segment vehicle with 75 kWh battery, and applies 8% annual fuel price inflation and 8% maintenance cost growth. All raw data files (CSV/JSON) and Python processing scripts are stored in the /data/ directory of this repository and are fully reproducible.
